Page 4: Power Factor Improvement in Three-Phase Systems

This page explains why power factor matters, how capacitor banks improve it, and how to size reactive compensation in a balanced three-phase installation.

Home Previous Why improve PF? Methods Simple example Complex example

1. Why improve power factor?

Lower current

For the same active power, a higher power factor reduces line current.

Lower losses

Reduced current means lower I²R losses in cables and transformers.

Better system capacity

Equipment can serve more useful power without being overloaded.

Caution: overcorrection can lead to a leading power factor and may cause voltage rise or resonance problems.

2. Common methods for improvement

  • Static capacitor banks connected at distribution boards or motor control centres.
  • Automatic power factor correction panels with stepped capacitors.
  • Synchronous condensers for large industrial systems.
  • Good load management to avoid lightly-loaded induction machines.
The most common practical method at low voltage is a capacitor bank, because capacitors supply leading reactive power which offsets lagging inductive reactive power.

3. Simple power factor improvement example

Problem: A balanced three-phase load absorbs P = 20 kW at an initial power factor of 0.75 lagging. Improve it to 0.95 lagging.

Initial tanφ₁ = tan(cos⁻¹ 0.75) = 0.8819 Final tanφ₂ = tan(cos⁻¹ 0.95) = 0.3287 Required capacitor kVAr: Qc = P (tanφ₁ - tanφ₂) Qc = 20 (0.8819 - 0.3287) = 11.06 kVAr
So a capacitor bank of about 11 kVAr is required.

4. More complex example: small industrial board

Suppose a three-phase board supplies three main groups of load:

  • Motor group: 30 kW at 0.78 lagging
  • Lighting: 6 kW at 0.95 lagging
  • Heating: 9 kW at unity power factor

Total active power:

P = 30 + 6 + 9 = 45 kW

Reactive powers:

Q₁ = 30 tan(cos⁻¹0.78) = 24.04 kVAr Q₂ = 6 tan(cos⁻¹0.95) = 1.97 kVAr Q₃ ≈ 0 kVAr Qtotal = 26.01 kVAr

Current overall PF:

S = √(45² + 26.01²) = 51.98 kVA PF = 45 / 51.98 = 0.866 lagging

If the target PF is 0.98:

Qtarget = P tan(cos⁻¹0.98) = 45 × 0.203 = 9.14 kVAr Qc = 26.01 - 9.14 = 16.87 kVAr
A practical choice would be an automatic capacitor bank of around 17 kVAr, often using stepped values.
Why this is more realistic: real installations often contain several load groups with different power factors. The engineer must combine the powers before selecting compensation.
Design caution: capacitor banks should be checked against harmonics, switching steps, and site operating patterns.
