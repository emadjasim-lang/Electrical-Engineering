Three-Phase Basics, Voltage Generation, and Waveforms

This page explains what a three-phase system is, how alternating voltage is generated, and why the three waveforms are 120° apart.

1. The basic idea of a three-phase system

In a single-phase system, there is one sinusoidal voltage. In a three-phase system, there are three sinusoidal voltages of equal magnitude and frequency. Each one reaches its peak at a different time, separated by 120 electrical degrees.

These three voltages are commonly labelled R, Y, B or A, B, C. Because the phases are evenly spaced, the total power delivered to a balanced load is much smoother than in single-phase supply.

Balanced system: equal phase voltages, equal phase impedances, and phase currents separated by 120°.

2. How a three-phase voltage is generated

A three-phase alternator has three stator windings placed physically 120° apart. When the rotor magnetic field rotates, an alternating emf is induced in each winding. Since the windings are displaced in space, the generated voltages are also displaced in time.

eR = Em sin(ωt) eY = Em sin(ωt - 120°) eB = Em sin(ωt - 240°)
If the machine is symmetrical, the three phase voltages have equal magnitude and the same frequency.

4. Benefits of a three-phase system

1. Constant power transfer

In a balanced three-phase system, the total instantaneous power is much more constant than in single-phase systems.

2. Better for motors

Three-phase motors are self-starting, efficient, and provide smooth torque.

3. Economy of conductors

For a given amount of power, less conductor material is needed than an equivalent single-phase arrangement.

Simple example: Three heaters supplied from three balanced phases share the load more evenly than one single-phase supply carrying the same total power.

5. Quick check

Question: Why are three-phase voltages separated by 120°?

Because the three stator windings in the generator are spaced 120 electrical degrees apart, so the induced voltages are also displaced by 120°.
