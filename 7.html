1. Main three-phase power equations

For a balanced three-phase system using line values:

Apparent power: S = √3 VLIL (VA) Active power: P = √3 VLILcosφ (W) Reactive power: Q = √3 VLILsinφ (var) Power factor: cosφ = P / S
The angle φ is the angle between phase voltage and phase current. A lagging power factor usually indicates an inductive load.
Active power P

This is the useful power converted into work, heat, or motion.

Reactive power Q

This oscillates between the source and reactive elements such as coils and capacitors.

Apparent power S

This is the total volt-ampere demand seen by the supply.

Power triangle: S² = P² + Q²

2. Simple worked example

Question: A balanced three-phase motor takes 400 V, 20 A at a power factor of 0.8 lagging. Find S, P, and Q.

S = √3 × 400 × 20 = 13.86 kVA P = √3 × 400 × 20 × 0.8 = 11.09 kW sinφ = √(1 - 0.8²) = 0.6 Q = √3 × 400 × 20 × 0.6 = 8.31 kVAr

This example is simple because only one balanced load is involved and the line values are already known.

3. More complex example: mixed load interpretation

Consider a small workshop supplied by a 400 V three-phase system. The workshop contains a motor group demanding 18 kW at 0.78 lagging power factor and heating loads demanding 6 kW at unity power factor.

The total active power is simply added:

Ptotal = 18 + 6 = 24 kW

The reactive power of the heaters is approximately zero because their power factor is close to 1. For the motor group:

tanφ = √(1 - 0.78²) / 0.78 = 0.8013 Qmotor = P tanφ = 18 × 0.8013 = 14.42 kVAr

So the total reactive power is about 14.42 kVAr, and the total apparent power becomes:

S = √(24² + 14.42²) = 28.00 kVA

Then the overall power factor is:

cosφ = P/S = 24 / 28 = 0.857 lagging
Why this is more complex: the load is not a single device. The engineer must combine active and reactive power from different parts of the installation.
Engineering meaning: even when some loads are resistive, inductive machines can still make the whole system run at a lagging power factor.
